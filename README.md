# GBM Confidence-Enhanced Survival Inference Project

An advanced genomic data pipeline and statistical framework designed to evaluate the independent prognostic value of high-risk genetic alterations in Glioblastoma (GBM). This project utilizes public genomic datasets (TCGA via cBioPortal) to assess how individual and combined molecular biomarkers impact overall survival (OS) and disease-free survival (DFS).

The project features a rigorous **Evidence Integration Framework** that scores and classifies biomarkers into distinct clinical confidence tiers based on their consistency across traditional survival models, causal propensity score adjustments, and machine learning algorithm.

---

## Clinical Scope & Target Biomarkers

Following professional clinical consultation, this workflow evaluates a curated panel of genomic alterations known to drive malignant progression or define diagnostic boundaries in high-grade gliomas:

| Category | Genetic Alteration | Clinical Implication |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Core Diagnostic Markers** | `IDH1` / `IDH2` (Wildtype) <br> `TERT` Promoter | Defines GBM (Grade 4), highly aggressive phenotype, poor prognosis. |
| **Tumor Suppressor Loss** | `TP53` <br> `PTEN` <br> `CDKN2A/B` | Loss of genomic stability, uninhibited cellular proliferation, progression. |
| **Oncogenic Signaling** | `EGFR` Amplification/Mutation | Drives aggressive phenotype and accelerated cell replication. |
| **Secondary Biomarkers** | `BRAF`, `PDGFRA`, `H3F3A` | Associated with high-grade biology and therapeutic resistance. |

## Pipeline Framework

The project is executed sequentially across 7 key analytical phases:
### Step 1: Cohort Characterization
* **Objective:** Clean raw datasets, isolate the 7 core high-risk genes (`ATRX`, `TERT`, `BRAF`, `EGFR`, `TP53`, `IDH1`, `IDH2`), filter out missing clinical endpoints, and validate baseline demographics against published GBM landmark literature.
* **Outputs:** Cleaned master dataset (`gbm_cleaned_merged_data.csv`), descriptive patient baseline characteristics table (Table 1), and target mutation frequency plots.

### Step 2: Single-Mutation Survival Analysis
* **Objective:** Isolate individual mutation groups against wildtype cohorts to estimate survival probabilities over time.
* **Methodology:** Kaplan-Meier (KM) survival curve generation and log-rank hypothesis testing.

### Step 3: Adjusted Cox Proportional Hazards Modeling
* **Objective:** Determine which genetic alterations provide independent prognostic survival tracking when adjusting for confounding clinical factors.
* **Methodology:** Unadjusted and multivariate Cox regression modeling incorporating covariates such as age, sex, and tumor stage. Summarized via hazard ratio tables and forest plots.

### Step 4: Propensity Score Enhanced Analysis (Causal Inference)
* **Objective:** Verify that mutation-survival associations remain robust and free from demographic sample assignment bias.
* **Methodology:** Propensity score estimation via Logistic Regression, Inverse Probability of Treatment Weighting (IPTW), Standardized Mean Difference (SMD) balance validation ($SMD < 0.10$), and weighted Cox regression models.

### Step 5: Co-Mutation Analysis
* **Objective:** Uncover phenotypic combinations of mutations that alter clinical risk profiles more severely than single alterations alone.
* **Target Configurations:** `TP53+EGFR`, `TP53+IDH1`, `ATRX+IDH1`, and `TERT+EGFR` analyzed across 4-group clinical splits.

### Step 6: Survival Machine Learning
* **Objective:** Leverage non-linear machine learning models to capture highly complex genomic feature interactions and rank biomarker predictive importance.
* **Methodology:** Random Survival Forests (RSF) and Gradient Boosted Survival Models (XGBoost Survival) evaluated using the Concordance Index (C-index).

### Step 7: Confidence Framework Integration
* **Objective:** Consolidate data points from Steps 2 through 6 to assign a final reproducible reproducibility score to each individual biomarker.
* **Classification Tiers:** Biomarkers are ranked into High, Moderate, or Low Clinical Confidence based on hazard ratio stability and statistical sign convergence across all methodologies.
